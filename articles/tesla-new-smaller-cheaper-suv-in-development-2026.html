Tesla Is Quietly Working on a Smaller, Cheaper SUV. Here's What We Know.

nexusEVnews April 10, 2026
Reported by Reuters on April 9, 2026, based on four people with knowledge of the project. Tesla did not respond to requests for comment. This article is an independent summary of those reported facts.

Tesla appears to be in early development on a new compact electric SUV, one that would be both smaller in size and lower in price than anything the company currently sells. Four people familiar with the project described the effort to Reuters, with the report published April 9, 2026.

What It Is

The vehicle would be an all-new design, not a variation of the Model 3 or Model Y. Tesla has reportedly been in contact with suppliers in recent weeks to discuss component specifications and manufacturing details. The four sources describe the project as still in an early development stage, and Reuters was not able to confirm that Tesla has formally approved the vehicle for production.

Size and Specs

Two of the sources put the vehicle's length at 4.28 meters. That's meaningfully shorter than the Model Y, which measures about 4.79 meters. To keep costs down, Tesla is said to be planning a smaller battery pack (which would mean less range than the Model Y's current 306 to 327 miles), a single electric motor rather than the dual-motor setup offered on current models, and a target weight of around 1.5 metric tons, compared to roughly 2 metric tons for the Model Y.

Where It Would Be Made

Three of the four sources say the vehicle would be built in China, most likely at Tesla's Shanghai factory. One of those sources adds that Tesla also intends to expand production to the United States and Europe, though no timeline was given for that expansion.

What It Would Cost

The price is expected to come in substantially below Tesla's current entry-level Model 3, which starts at $34,000 in China and around $37,000 in the United States. The sources did not name a specific target price. Production is unlikely to start in 2026.

Context

This news comes after Tesla scrapped a separate, long-anticipated low-cost EV project in 2024. That project, widely referred to as the "Model 2" by investors and media, had been positioned as Tesla's answer to the mass-market EV segment. Tesla pivoted instead toward robotaxis and autonomous systems. When more affordable versions of existing models did arrive -- a standard Model 3 at $36,990 and a standard Model Y at $39,990 -- they did not produce a significant change in Tesla's overall sales trajectory.

Tesla's Cybercab robotaxi is entering production this month. As of the Reuters report, Tesla had not filed for the federal exemption that would be required to sell the Cybercab -- which has no steering wheel or pedals -- in the United States. Tesla's market capitalization stands at approximately $1.3 trillion.

Tesla did not respond to Reuters' questions about any new vehicle plans.
